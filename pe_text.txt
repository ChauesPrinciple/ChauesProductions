Open Wounds
Objective: Create realistic open wounds for close-up shots.
Materials:
Glue (non-toxic school glue works well)
Toilet paper
Standard makeup items (foundation, red lipstick, black eyeshadow)
Fake blood (from above recipe)
Steps:
Apply a thin layer of glue to the skin where the wound will go.
Place a small piece of toilet paper on top and let it dry partially.
Apply additional glue on top, shaping the toilet paper to create raised edges or tears.
Blend foundation or skin-colored makeup over the dried glue and paper to match the skin tone.
Add red lipstick or red makeup inside the wound to mimic fresh tissue.
Dab black eyeshadow around the edges for depth.
Finish with fake blood for realism.
Filming Tips:
Use close-up shots to emphasize the wound detail.
Gore Realism Enhancements

Mix sand or fine gravel into fake blood for gritty, coagulated effects.
Use oatmeal soaked in blood for chunky or clotted appearances.
Layering:
Apply thin layers of gelatin over fake blood to create "skin" peeling effects.
Add translucent silicone sheets to simulate deeper layers of flesh.
Temperature Effects:
Warm fake blood for realistic fluidity.
Chill gelatin for stiffer, aged wounds.
Add subtle lighting from the side to highlight textures.

Dry Ice Smoke (Classic Fog)
Materials:
Dry ice (available at grocery or specialty stores)
Warm water (not boiling, around 60Â°C/140Â°F)
Insulated gloves or tongs (to handle dry ice safely)
Plastic or metal container
Optional: Fans or tubing for control
Steps:
Fill the container with warm water (approximately halfway full).
Using gloves or tongs, carefully add a small piece of dry ice into the water.
The dry ice will sublimate (turn directly into gas), producing dense, low-lying fog.
To sustain the effect, add more warm water as it cools or periodically refresh the water and dry ice.
For directed smoke, attach a flexible tube to the container and guide the flow.
Practical Tips:
Use shallow containers for ground-level effects and tall containers for columns.
For outdoor use, ensure minimal wind for better smoke control.
Smoke from Incense or Smoke Pellets
Materials:
Incense sticks or cones
Smoke pellets (available from theatrical or hobby stores)
Metal tray or fire-safe surface
Steps:
Place the incense or smoke pellet on a fire-safe surface.
Light the incense or pellet and allow it to smolder, producing a steady stream of smoke.
Adjust placement or combine multiple sources for thicker smoke effects.
Use a handheld fan to guide the smoke in desired directions or create motion.
Practical Tips:
Ideal for smaller, controlled smoke sources like steaming pipes or mystical effects.
Incense offers various scents, which can help mitigate discomfort for actors.
Paper and Sawdust Smoke (Old-School Fire Simulation)
Materials:
Torn paper or cardboard
Fine sawdust
Metal tray or fire-safe container
Matches or a lighter
Steps:
Place the paper and sawdust into the metal tray.
Light the edge of the paper and allow it to burn briefly before extinguishing it.
The smoldering materials will release thick, dark smoke.
Add more sawdust to sustain the effect as needed.
Practical Tips:
Works well for simulating burning debris or aftermath of a fire.
Use outdoors or in well-ventilated areas due to strong odors.
Haze in a Can (Commercial Smoke Spray)
Materials:
Haze in a Can (available at theatrical supply stores or online)
Steps:
Shake the can thoroughly before use.

Cotton Wick Smoke (Handheld or Small Area Effects)
Materials:
Cotton rope or thick string
Small fireproof dish
Vegetable oil or candle wax
Steps:
Soak the cotton rope in oil or melted candle wax.
Allow it to dry partially until it is tacky.
Light one end of the rope and blow out the flame to produce smoke.
Move the smoldering rope around the scene for localized smoke effects.
Practical Tips:
Works well for mystical or ritualistic settings.
Use in a fire-safe environment and extinguish thoroughly after use.

Exercise: Creating a Realistic Prosthetic Hand
Objective: Make a life-like severed hand for horror scenes.
Materials:
Alginate molding powder
Water
Two mixing buckets
Mixing stick (or paint mixer drill attachment)
Silicone rubber (trial-size kit)
Silicone paint (with flesh tones and tints)
Steps:
Prepare the Mold:
Mix alginate molding powder and water in a 1:1 ratio in a bucket.
Stir quickly (it hardens in 5 minutes) until the consistency is smooth.
Submerge your hand into the mixture in the desired position.
Wait 10-15 minutes for the mold to harden. Wiggle your hand free carefully.
Pour the Silicone:
Combine the two parts of silicone rubber according to the kit instructions.
Pour the mixture into the hand mold, ensuring it fills every crevice.
Let the silicone set for 5-6 hours.
Paint and Detail:
Remove the prosthetic from the mold.
Use silicone paint to match a realistic skin tone.
Add details like veins, bruises, or blood using red, white, and blue tones.
Filming:
Frame the hand in close-ups to highlight realistic textures.
Position it on a surface with fake blood for an added gore effect.
