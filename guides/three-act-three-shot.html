Three-Act, Three-Shot Guide

The Structure of Story

Most stories can be understood as a sequence of three major movements. Like a magic trick, the structure works because it guides attention, sets expectations, and then changes the situation in a way that forces a resolution.

It's a framework, not a cage. The structure stays consistent (Setup → Escalation → Resolution), while what you build inside it can vary endlessly in genre, tone, and style.

At its core, three-act structure is about plot and transformation. The “story” is what happens. The “plot” is how those events are arranged and revealed.

Act One: Setup and Commitment

Act One establishes the baseline world. It gives the audience a reason to keep watching, then introduces the central problem.

  • The Hook: Why should we watch? Often creates curiosity or begins "in the middle of something."
  • The Inciting Event: The disruption that changes "normal" for the protagonist.
  • Plot Point 1: The threshold moment. The protagonist is committed to the journey and cannot return to normal.

Act Two: Escalation and Reversal

The sustain struggle. The protagonist faces obstacles, pressure increases, and the story tests their commitment.

  • Midpoint: A structural pivot where the conflict becomes clearer, more personal, or unavoidable. The protagonist often shifts from reactive to proactive.
  • Pinch Points: Moments that clarify the risk and remind us of the antagonistic force.

Act Three: Crisis and Resolution

The story cashes out everything established so far.

  • Climax: The decisive confrontation that determines the outcome. It fulfills the protagonist's transformation.
  • Resolution: A brief landing to show the "new normal" and settle consequences.

The Three-Shot Worksheet

To master this structure, we practice with the smallest possible narrative unit: The Three-Shot Sequence. We map the three acts directly onto three shots.
